One of my favourite projects and an honour to be the Carpenter for the project. Originally concieved as a joint venture between Hill Holt architecture student Chris Myk and local community Archaologist Emily Gillott and a community volunteer project at Skylarks Nature reserve in Nottingham. Remains of various structures had been found and excavated on site and from the remains we collaborated to design what we thought could have been built on the site during Saxon times. I shall not go into too much detail on the archaology as I'm no expert, but it was interesting speaking to the archaeologists about details they'd found in old timber frames that still directly relates to the way we do things today.

The plan was to reconstruct a building from the ground up usind mainly tools and techniques available at the time, I did not construct everything entirely, side axeing all the round poles into square would have taken an eternity, so we did the 8 oak posts using a side axe and the rest of the frame was processed on the modern saw mill. The rest of the frame details were cut using the same techniques and tools I still use today, where today the only additions I would use would be a chain morticer replacing hand augers and large circular saws save time compared to hand saws, although most of the time when using green timber a good hand saw can be equally effective.

The timber was all sourced and feeled locally by our team, at the time we were managing local woodlands for the councils on a project to restore our woodlands to native species, so following the War and the decimation of our native woodlands, it ws decided to replace what was lost in many cases by faster growing pine species, this ultimately led to loss in bio-diversity due to pine forrest floors supporting a lot less of our native flora, fauna and funghi. Projects supported by the National Lottery funded us to remove and process the mostly Scotts Pine whilst replanting with native species such as Ash, Birch and Oak. The standing posts are Oak burned and tarred at the ground intersection as per archaological evidence, and although traditionally the whole fram would have probably been of Oak, we decided to use the more abundant and ecologically sound Scotts Pine as it would ultimately not be in too much direct weather and so it's relative lack of durability was less of an issue. The shingle roof is cedar shingel which were sourced locally, again although we could have produced them ourselves the timescale would have been prohibitive.

# Stage 2 - Finishing and Details

****

Following the completion of the frame and the topping off ceremony in the Autumn of 2017 the project was left over the winter months, the frame settling naturally before we completed the infill and doors the following spring. The doors I madde from local Douglas Fir, chosen for its durability and availability, the frame was holed for integrated hinges made from the small amount of remaining Oak we had left over from the main Frame. I decided to use the oak as I wanted the wooden hinge to last as long as possible. We had no idea what technique would have been used at the time, but no evidence of metalwork was found at the dig site to suggest metal hinges and latches so I dry pinned the green timber boards and braces together, carved nubs on the ends of the hinge pieces and fashioned a coulpe of latches before installing them on the building. It all worked flawlessly and have held up well over the years.

After the doors were complete, technically my work was done and we handed the project over to the volunteer team to complete the walls, however, I couldn't resist and returned to volunteer for a wall construction day. As shown the walls are constructed from woven Willow and Hazel coppice before beaing daubed in a very local mud, clay and hair mix. Essentially a wattle and daub wall. A lot of fun was had over the course of this project, as well as some very late nights in the workshop getting the frame cut in time, it may not be obvious from the timelapse, but I get a lot of interuptions, some welcome, others more frustrating! This was whilst I worked at Hill Holt Wood but prior to me teaching, and if I remember some of the interuptions were the learners, and ultimately the beginning of a new chapter. I would like to thank all those involved for their opportunity, support and understanding.

## Some interesting reflections post build

- Most of the build I did by myself in under 6 months, you would need friends to help and a small team of maybe 6 people could construct this entire building easily.
- Skills required are relatively simple, most fit joints are not essential for construction rigidity and any air gaps can always be plugged with daub.
- Tools required are very minimal, a saw, couple of chisels an auger and axes and adzes as well as some basic marking tools, most of which you can fashion from twine and straight lumber.
- The material costs are about a dozen medium trees, some durable for post in the ground and shingles need to repel water, although alternatives such as thatch or maybe green roofs could be substituted depending on the resources available.
- Wall structure is local mud, probably horse, human and other animal hair tied into woven copice, either hazel which could be split or willow harvested young.
- Regular maintenace would include whitewashing the daub, lime putty watered down and applied in many thin coats as well as oiling the wood to prevent drying and cracking whilst maintaining durability.
- The resultant building bares a lot in common with modern eco principles, the materials are all locally sourced, naturally breathable and very low carbon impact.
- Teams of volunteers or communities come together to help with certain tasks, learning new skills from each other along the way.
