# Finally upgrading the staircase at Boggart Hall

## A project for my friend Grace.

****

I met Grace many years ago during what I now call a recharge part of my life, following stressful times and a reset a friend recommended I try a website called [the poosh dot org](http://www.thepoosh.org/), which at the time, (it seems to have shut down now), offered a similar service to woofing (working on organic farms) so you would offer your service in exchange for bed and board, or whatever arrangement suits all parties. So I ended up in Todmorden on a concrete slab cutting huge larch timber frames in exchange for wonderful cakes and buiscuits and a warm dry space to sleep. I intended to stay for a couple of months, get the frame up, meet Barbara Jones of [Straworks](https://strawworks.co.uk/) and [School of Natural Building](https://schoolofnaturalbuilding.co.uk/) and learn a little about Straw Bale building. This turned out to be almost 18 months initially, staying on to help with the roof structure and a few other bits before setting off to work with Barbara Jones on her next big project, I did however continue to return throughout the following years, each time returning to help on the next room, the kitchen, bathroom, office etc. always in exchange for sustanance and warmth. My time over the years with Grace allowed me to live as close as possible to money free and I can safely say that without bills and payslips, tax returns and all the other ridiculous buraucracy I felt about as stress free as I ever have as an adult. If reading this you get an opportunity to travel and meet the people you do volunteering I highly recomend it.

Grace has an extensive blog herself she kept whilst building her wonderful house, I feature a fair bit! and am eternally thankful for the opportunities this exposure gave me, you can read the whole thing here: [donated house dot org](https://donatedhouse.org.uk/) It was on one of my returns around the end of 2021 that I started the project, and completed the structure before returning 2023 for the cladding and finishing.

****

# Stage 1 - Planning

First job is to measure up and do many calculations, for me I tend to use CAD software for larger projects, initiated in CAD first drawing a car on a ZX spectrum using basic draw and move commands to produce a very primitive line drawing resembling a car. From there I first used autoCAD in the mid 90's when mice were only just beginning to appear and most commands were still fairly similar to my first childhood experience on the Spectrum. Over the years I have continued to use various packages, including a hyperfocused session over lockdown on Blender modelling, see [Blender Rabbithole](/content/new2.md) This planning was done using Fusion360 a student version is available for free with limited feratures, otherwise it is proffesional software and far too expensive for the level at which I use the software. Currently I have discovered the virtues of FOSS (free open source software) and FreeCAD but another rabbit hole I shall not venture down here!

The measurements are input and then I can draw the structure and details in allowing the software to then produce working drawings for me, doing all the calculations required in the background. In theory this should allow for testing and visualising the finished product, allowing me to make changes before committing to actual time and materials, unfortunately, as can be identified in the series of photographs and videos, I failed to notice the height clearance on the first step and the horizontal post running above before I started constructing the bottom few steps. The most painful part as you'll see is I actually spent the time producing a walkthrough animations, focusing the camera at the floor as I programmed the decent of the last few steps, if I'd have used a higher view angle it probably would have been obvious. The solution to the problem was to introbuce the kite steps, splitting the turn between the top and the bottom.

****

# Stage 2 - Jig making, mockup and assembly

****

With the calculations and drawings done my first job is mocking up the first few steps, I spent a couple of days carcassing this together, only to discover anyone above average height would clock their head on the beam, I was so proud I took progress pictures before I realised. For some reason I didn't take any pictures of the solution prior to cladding, so I'm keeping these here to remind myself to have an extra think when using virtual environments for designing.

I created a fairly simple jig from the drawings which I could use a follower router bit with half the jig before moving it to the next step and repeating the process until I had the reqired number of steps for each stringer, the inner being closed and outer open, a design choice to allow a flat surface to finish the render on the straw walls whilst keeping the outer open to bullnose and carry the herringbone design along coherently. The Stringers here are made from salvaged timber, I stripped maybe 200 nails out of these pieces of wood and had to dowell 2 lengths together to get the required width, the plywood some screws and glue were all that was purchased for this whole project, the cladding having been salvaged years ago from school gym floors and the Newel posts and banister from a lightning struck Rowan tree cut down the year before in the garden.

# Stage 3 - Cladding & Hand Rail

After a break and another job, I returned to clad the staircase with the reclaimed maple gym flooring Grace had been hoarding for years and make and fit the Newel post and banister from the various bits of trunk and branches from the Rowan tree from the garden. A lot of cutting, fitting fettling and nail removal were requred for this job. the boarder and bull nose detail created by cutting tounges off the length before applying the rounded profile, each board also has a diagonal tongue re-cut to mesh with the boarder pieces and provide mechanical strength and stop the board twisting free. The Newel post is then cleaned and carved to fit the profile of the steps and I carved a simple acorn fineal on top, leaving the traditional knife marks rather than sanding smooth to compliment the natural look.

# Finished... for now!

The flooring was continued up and around onto the first floor landing and down one little step, nothing too elabourate here, just cutting and matching the old boards. There were 2 separate batches of flooring, some with adhesive that needed to be cleaned and the other with nails. They were also subtly different around the tongue and groove section, so a router was used on each of the grooves that intersected between types to allow a flush finish without having to sand as the character of the boards including the old paint marks were to be kept to tell the story. A lot of labour is required to produce this from mostly recycled materials, but the results to me are worth it.
